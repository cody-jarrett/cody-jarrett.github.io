Phillips Research Experience Research Assistant Phillips Laboratory Institute of Ecology and Evolution, University of Oregon, Eugene, OR 97403 September 2018 – Present As a research assistant in the highly interdisciplinary and collaborative Phillips Lab, I play a role in many projects. I provide custom equipment engineering, construction, programming, and maintenance, while also providing general IT and hardware support for the biologists studying aging and reproduction in the model nematode Caenorhabditis elegans. In my most recent project, I created a custom thermal control and imaging apparatus for C. elegans reproductive phenotyping called the Egg Counter. This project also had me developing image analysis pipelines to detect worm eggs using neural networks, using decision tree classifiers to classify eggs vs. larvae, using a high-performance computing cluster to simulate experimental data to inform the experimentalists on experimental design, applying statistical distribution modeling techniques, and developing various analyses and visualizations of the data collected from the equipment. I also created a series of GUI applications which form a data collection, processing, and annotation pipeline for equipment users to prep and collect data for final storage and analysis. To support the lab’s research into the biology of aging, I monitor and maintain their flatbed scanner arrays and Linux-based image acquisition servers. I have also set up fresh Linux servers and provided IT support for labs at Rutgers University and The Buck Institute as part of their collaborative CITP aging project. Research Assistant Jaramillo Lab Institute of Neuroscience, University of Oregon December 2018 – August 2019 While in the Jaramillo lab, I engaged in programming and custom device creation, and provided general IT support for researchers studying auditory cognition using mice as a model organism. Specifically, I adjusted Python and Arduino software the PI had written to make it work with devices that detected when mice would lick a metal tube and that measured the motion of a
Cody M. Jarrett 2 mouse running on a wheel. I also designed and 3D printed components for the lick detector. Additionally, I learned KiCAD to re-design a collaborator’s circuit for the lick detector so that it could be adapted to the Jaramillo lab’s equipment and built a sound and light isolation box for a two-photon microscope. Undergraduate Researcher Phillips Laboratory Institute of Ecology and Evolution, University of Oregon, Eugene, OR 97403 August 2015 – June 2017 As an undergraduate researcher I independently learned and used electronics, PCB design, and Python programming skills to design, assemble, and program custom 16-channel temperature recorders for standardizing temperature-dependent data between the Phillips Lab and labs at Rutgers University and the Buck Institute as part of their collaborative CITP aging project. I also constructed and programmed a custom environment monitoring and alarm system for a climate-controlled room using a Raspberry Pi with various sensors.
